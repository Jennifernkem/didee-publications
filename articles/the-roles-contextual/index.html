The Roles of Contextual Instructional Models in Addressing Misconceptions Held by Secondary School Physics Students

Kingsley T. Onah
Department of Science Education, Enugu State University of Science and Technology
Published: March 1, 2026
Volume: 1, Issue: 4
Pages: 131-145

Abstract

This study examines the pivotal role of contextual models in addressing and demystifying common misconceptions in secondary school physics. Physics concepts, often abstract, stem from misconceptions that conflict with students' intuitive understandings rooted in everyday experiences, leading to persistent low achievement. Contextual models, by embedding scientific principles within relatable real-world scenarios, facilitate deeper and more accurate conceptual understanding. They help students bridge the gap between pre-existing notions and scientific explanations by demonstrating practical application and relevance of physics. This approach fosters cognitive conflict, prompting students to critically evaluate existing ideas and reconstruct knowledge based on scientific principles, ultimately enhancing conceptual clarity, promoting critical thinking, and improving students' ability to apply physics effectively.

Introduction

Physics is a natural science involving the study of matter, motion, energy, and force, serving as the most fundamental root of every field of science. As a core subject in secondary schools, physics contributes to cognitive development and forms the basis for technological advancement and human resource development. However, students often enter classrooms with deeply rooted misconceptions—alternative or naive conceptions that deviate from scientifically accepted ideas. These misconceptions stem from everyday experiences, intuitive reasoning, or misinterpretations of scientific concepts, hindering deep understanding and effective application of physics in the real world.

Understanding Misconceptions in Physics

Misconceptions in physics are persistent, incorrect beliefs that students hold, often context-dependent and arising from everyday experiences or intuitive reasoning. These are not mere errors but stable, deeply rooted cognitive frameworks that students develop based on prior experiences. For instance, students may believe heavier objects fall faster due to gravity, a misconception rooted in intuitive observations. Research indicates that misconceptions reflect fragmented or context-specific knowledge structures that lack integration with expert-like understanding. These alternative conceptions are resistant to change, often persisting even after traditional instruction, making their identification and remediation critical for effective physics education.

Types of Misconceptions

Misconceptions can be categorized into five key types: Factual misconceptions involve incorrect beliefs about specific facts, such as thinking Earth is the only body exerting gravitational force in the solar system. Conceptual misconceptions represent misunderstandings of fundamental principles, like believing an object at rest has no forces acting on it, ignoring balanced forces. Procedural misconceptions involve errors in applying physics concepts to problem-solving, such as misinterpreting vector addition in force diagrams. Ontological misconceptions occur when students assign incorrect categories to physical entities, such as treating heat as a substance rather than energy transfer. Context-specific misconceptions are beliefs that hold true in one context but not another, such as assuming gravity does not act in fluids because objects appear to float in water.

Contextual Instructional Models

Contextual instructional models are schemas, graphical representations, and mind mapping frameworks that relate subject matter to real-life situations and environments. These models embed physics concepts within real-world scenarios or problems relevant to students' lives or interests. Unlike de-contextualized problems focusing purely on abstract principles, contextual models tie concepts to practical situations, such as calculating the motion of a car or forces in sports activities. The eight characteristics of contextual models include making meaningful connections, doing significant work, self-regulated learning, collaboration, critical and creative thinking, nurturing individuals, reaching high standards, and using authentic assessment. By grounding abstract ideas in familiar contexts, these models enhance engagement, motivation, and conceptual understanding.

Roles in Addressing Misconceptions

Contextual instructional models play five key roles in addressing misconceptions. First, they enhance conceptual understanding by connecting abstract physics principles to tangible scenarios, helping students integrate fragmented knowledge into coherent cognitive structures. Second, they facilitate cognitive conflict by presenting scenarios that contradict students' naive beliefs, encouraging them to confront and revise existing ideas. Third, they promote engagement and motivation by leveraging real-world relevance to increase student interest. Fourth, they support model-based learning by emphasizing scientific models to represent and explain phenomena, allowing students to visualize and manipulate abstract concepts. Fifth, they address equity and inclusivity by being tailored to diverse cultural and socio-economic contexts, incorporating culturally relevant examples that make physics accessible to students from varied backgrounds.

Theoretical Framework

The study is grounded in Jean Piaget's Constructivist Learning Theory, which posits that learning is an active, constructive process where individuals build new knowledge based on existing cognitive structures. Key tenets include that learners actively construct understanding by interacting with their environment, knowledge is built through experiences that challenge existing mental frameworks, and learning occurs through assimilation and accommodation. Misconceptions arise when students assimilate incorrect information into their schemas, and learning requires accommodation to correct these inaccuracies. In secondary school physics, contextual instructional models serve as powerful tools aligned with constructivist principles by providing tangible, relatable, and interactive experiences that facilitate active engagement, challenge incorrect schemas, and promote accommodation of scientific concepts.

Challenges and Limitations

Despite their benefits, contextual instructional models face several challenges. Teachers often overestimate the motivational impact of contextualized problems, particularly for older students who prefer abstract problems. The design of contextual models requires careful consideration to avoid overwhelming students with overly complex scenarios that could reinforce rather than resolve misconceptions. Poorly designed contextual problems can confuse students if the real-world context obscures the underlying physics principle. Models often struggle to generalize effectively across the wide range of misconceptions students may have, as they rely on predefined patterns that may not encompass the full spectrum of erroneous beliefs. Additionally, teachers' limited pedagogical content knowledge can hinder effective implementation, and contextual models typically lack the ability to engage in dynamic, interactive dialogues necessary for iterative questioning and adaptive learning.

Conclusion

Contextual instructional models play a multifaceted role in demystifying misconceptions in secondary school physics by enhancing conceptual understanding, facilitating cognitive conflict, promoting engagement, supporting model-based learning, and addressing equity. While challenges such as teacher training and design complexity persist, strategic implementation through curriculum design, teacher training, and technology integration can maximize their effectiveness. By embedding scientific principles within relatable real-world scenarios, contextual models help students bridge the gap between pre-existing notions and scientific explanations, fostering cognitive conflict that prompts critical evaluation and knowledge reconstruction based on scientific principles.
